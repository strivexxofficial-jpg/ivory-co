Chapter One

What a veneer
actually is.

A dental veneer is a thin shell of ceramic or composite material, custom-fabricated to bond to the facial (front) surface of a tooth. At its most fundamental, it functions as a precision replacement for the visual layer of your enamel — altering colour, shape, length, and surface texture while preserving the underlying tooth structure.

The concept dates to 1928, when California dentist Charles Pincus developed temporary facings for Hollywood film sets — held in place with denture adhesive for the duration of a shoot. Modern veneers, by contrast, are bonded with light-cured resin cements achieving bond strengths in excess of 20 MPa — comparable to the tensile strength of natural enamel.

What separates an exceptional veneer from a mediocre one is not the material alone — it is the interplay between the laboratory's characterisation skill, the preparation margin design, and the cement shade selection. A veneer case is a three-way collaboration between patient, clinician, and ceramist.

Chapter Two

The materials
science.

Not all veneers are the same material. The three dominant options — feldspathic porcelain, lithium disilicate (IPS e.max), and composite resin — each carry different mechanical properties, optical behaviours, and clinical applications. Understanding the distinction prevents the most common veneer regret: receiving the wrong material for the wrong case.

Option 01 — Gold Standard

Feldspathic Porcelain

Hand-layered by ceramists. Unmatched optical depth — light transmission virtually identical to natural enamel. The material of choice for anterior cases where aesthetics is the primary objective.

Thickness0.3–0.5mm
Flexural strength60–90 MPa
Longevity15–25 years
Prep requiredMinimal–none
TranslucencyExceptional
Option 02 — Most Durable

Lithium Disilicate (e.max)

CAD/CAM pressed ceramic. Superior fracture resistance — three times stronger than feldspathic. Preferred for patients with parafunctional habits (bruxism) or posterior applications.

Thickness0.4–0.8mm
Flexural strength360–400 MPa
Longevity15–20 years
Prep requiredMinimal
TranslucencyGood
Option 03 — Entry Point

Composite Resin

Direct or indirect. Chairside-placed or lab-fabricated. Lower upfront cost but requires polishing maintenance, stains over time, and typically replaces in 5–8 years. Useful for trial cases.

Thickness1.0–2.0mm

Our Recommendation

How We Choose

Material selection is driven by: existing tooth shade, occlusal forces, patient parafunctional history, and the opacity needed to achieve the target shade. There is no universal answer — there is the right material for your specific case.

We use exclusively IPS e.max and Vita PM9 feldspathic from two ceramists with combined 40+ years of anterior cosmetic experience.

Chapter Three

Your clinical
timeline.

A well-executed veneer case is never completed in a single appointment. What follows is the honest clinical sequence — the steps most practices abbreviate in consultation and the reasons each one is non-negotiable.

1
Week 1
Comprehensive Aesthetic Assessment
Photographs, full-arch impressions or digital scan (iTero), shade mapping, and bite analysis. We assess gingival symmetry, lip line dynamics during animation, midline deviation, and the golden proportion ratios. Any periodontal disease is treated before cosmetic work begins — no exceptions.
2
Week 1–2
Digital Smile Design & Wax-Up Approval
Using DSD (Digital Smile Design) software, we superimpose proposed tooth shapes onto your facial photographs and present three design options. A diagnostic wax-up is fabricated — physical models showing the proposed outcome in 3D. You approve the shape before any preparation begins.
3
Week 2–3
Tooth Preparation & Provisionals
Enamel reduction (0.3–0.5mm for feldspathic; 0.4–0.8mm for e.max) using a depth-cutting bur under magnification. Provisional veneers are placed immediately — you leave with functional, aesthetic temporaries while the ceramist fabricates finals. This is your trial period: live with the shape, adjust if needed.
4
Week 4–5
Try-In, Cementation & Occlusal Check
Final veneers are tried in dry (no cement) with try-in paste to verify shade. After patient approval, the enamel surface is etched, silanated, and the veneer is bonded with light-cured resin cement. Occlusal contacts are verified in excursive movements.
5
Month 3 & Annually
Review & Maintenance Protocol
A 3-month post-placement review examines marginal integrity, gingival response, and occlusal wear. Thereafter, annual reviews with professional polishing using non-abrasive prophylaxis paste maintain the surface glaze. Nightguard fabrication is strongly recommended for all veneer patients.

Ideal Candidates
  • ✓Intrinsic staining unresponsive to whitening (tetracycline, fluorosis, devitalised teeth)
  • ✓Minor shape irregularities — chips, worn edges, size discrepancies
  • ✓Mild spacing (diastemas under 3mm) where orthodontics has been declined
  • ✓Healthy periodontium and no active decay
  • ✓Stable occlusion with adequate enamel for bonding
When We Recommend Alternatives First
  • —Significant crowding — Invisalign first preserves tooth structure
  • —Active bruxism without a management plan — veneers will fracture
  • —Periodontal disease — compromises bond strength and gingival margins
  • —Patients under 18 — pulp chambers are larger; preparation risk elevated

Chapter Five

Longevity &
maintenance.

A 2019 systematic review in the Journal of Dentistry aggregated data from 64 clinical studies covering 8,973 veneers over 10–25 year periods.

95%5-year survival rate (feldspathic)
91%10-year survival rate
73%20-year survival (with maintenance)

The leading causes of veneer failure: fracture (34%), debonding (21%), marginal discolouration (18%), secondary caries (14%), periodontal complications (13%). All but fracture are preventable with proper maintenance.

Your Maintenance Protocol
  • →Daily: Non-abrasive fluoride toothpaste (RDA <70). Avoid charcoal or whitening formulas.
  • →Daily: Floss at margins. Bacterial biofilm is the primary driver of marginal staining.
  • →Nightly: Wear your nightguard. Porcelain in bruxism without protection has a 4x higher fracture rate.
  • →Bi-annually: Professional clean with non-abrasive prophy paste. No pumice, no air-polishing.
  • →Avoid: Biting nails, pens, or hard foods directly with veneered teeth. Ice and hard candy are the most common fracture triggers.

Chapter Six

Questions every
patient asks.

Will my teeth be sensitive after preparation?

Yes — for 1 to 6 weeks post-preparation in approximately 35–40% of patients. Prescription-strength fluoride varnish applied immediately post-prep significantly reduces this, and it resolves as the provisionals insulate the tooth. Persistent sensitivity beyond 8 weeks warrants investigation of pulpal health.

Can veneers be whitened if they discolour?

No. Ceramic and composite resin do not respond to peroxide-based whitening agents. The veneer shade is permanent from placement. For this reason, we recommend completing any desired tooth whitening 2–3 weeks before impression-taking, allowing the shade to stabilise before we match the veneer to it.

What happens when veneers need to be replaced?

Replacement is a planned eventuality, not a failure. A well-bonded veneer is removed under local anaesthetic, the surface is cleaned, and a new veneer is fabricated to the same design. Most patients undergo one replacement cycle in a lifetime if maintenance protocols are followed.

Are "no-prep" veneers clinically legitimate?

Conditionally. Ultra-minimal preparation feldspathic veneers on teeth with adequate space are clinically sound. However, "no-prep" is frequently applied to cases where some preparation is clinically indicated — creating an over-contoured result that traps food and compromises gingival health.

How many teeth should I veneer?

The smile zone — teeth visible during a natural, relaxed smile — is the functional boundary. For most patients, this is 8–10 upper teeth. Veneering fewer creates shade and shape discontinuities visible on close examination.
